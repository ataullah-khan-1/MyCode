Both my colleagues have explained the technical side really well. I just want to talk about something more straightforward — the real world reality of how we actually work day to day.
Let me just talk about one simple thing — time. GitHub's own research showed that developers finish tasks 55% faster when they use AI tools. That is real. That is significant. In our day to day work that means we can deliver more in the same amount of time. But here is the funny part — if every single time AI generates some code we stop and put it through a long manual review process, we just lost all that time we saved. Think about it. What was the point of using AI in the first place then. And this is not a small inefficiency. If you multiply that across a team of developers, across multiple projects, across an entire organisation — that delay adds up. The business impact is real and it is something we cannot ignore.
And it is not just about speed. When a developer writes code and then writes tests for that same code, they are only thinking about what they already thought of when writing it. They are not going to suddenly discover something they missed. That is just human nature. We all have blind spots. We all have assumptions baked into the way we think about a problem. But AI tools do not carry those same assumptions. They can generate test cases for all kinds of situations — empty values, null inputs, boundary conditions, unexpected combinations — things that honestly most of us would not think about in the middle of a busy day. So actually AI is not reducing quality here. It is improving it. It is covering ground that human written tests often leave uncovered. And that is the opposite of what the AGAINST side is arguing today.
Now let me bring in something that I think is really important for this debate. We are not talking about a small experiment here. Over 90% of Fortune 100 companies are already using GitHub Copilot today. Let that number sink in for a moment. Nine out of ten of the world's largest companies. These are not startups taking shortcuts. These are organisations with legal teams, compliance requirements, security audits, and quality standards that most of us can only imagine. They have gone through exactly this debate internally. They have asked exactly this question — is it acceptable to use AI generated code without putting every single line through a comprehensive independent human review. And their answer, backed by real results in real production environments, is yes. With the right automated checks and validation in place, it is absolutely acceptable. If companies of that size and with those standards have made that call, I think it is a strong signal for the rest of us.
And I think that is really what this debate comes down to. It is not about whether AI is perfect. Nothing is perfect. It is not about whether human reviewers add value. Of course they do in the right context. It is about whether making human review a mandatory comprehensive independent step for every single piece of AI generated code is actually making things safer — or whether it is just making things slower. The industry has looked at that question carefully and made its decision. AI generated code with proper automated validation is acceptable. The only question left for us today is whether we are going to move forward with confidence or keep adding manual steps that slow us down without meaningfully improving the outcome. At the end of the day trust in software has never come from who reviewed it. It has always come from whether it works. And if it works — it works.
